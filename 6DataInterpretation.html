Econometric Analysis of Labor Data: Effect of Education on Wages

Author

Mohan Khanal

Published

August 8, 2025

Introduction

This analysis investigates the impact of education on hourly wages, a core question in labor economics. Using the wage1 dataset from the wooldridge package in R, we estimate the effect of years of education on wages, controlling for work experience and job tenure. The dataset contains 526 observations from U.S. individuals in 1976. We employ Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression with robust standard errors to ensure reliable inference.

    • wage: Hourly wage in USD (dependent variable).

Methodology

  • Model: OLS regression:
    \[ \text{wage} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{educ} + \beta_2 \text{exper} + \beta_3 \text{tenure} + \epsilon \]
  • Assumptions: Linearity, no severe multicollinearity, homoscedasticity (tested), and exogeneity of regressors (assumed for simplicity).
  • Estimation: Robust standard errors address potential heteroscedasticity.

Coefficient Interpretation Coefficient Interpretation

Education ((_1 = 0.5414)): A one-year increase in education is associated with a \(0.5414\) increase in hourly wages, holding experience and tenure constant. This is statistically significant (p < 0.001) and economically meaningful, implying a ~9% increase relative to the mean wage ($5.90).

Experience ((_2 = 0.0418)): Each additional year of experience increases wages by $0.0418, significant at p < 0.001, consistent with human capital theory.

Tenure ((_3 = 0.0781)): A one-year increase in tenure raises wages by $0.0781 (p = 0.001), suggesting job-specific skills or loyalty matter.

Constant: Not statistically significant (p = 0.190), as expected for an intercept.

Fit: The model explains 31.6% of wage variation (( R^2 = 0.3161 )), reasonable for cross-sectional data but indicating other factors (e.g., ability, occupation) may play a role.

Results show a slight increase in ( R^2 ) to 0.328, with the education coefficient stable ((_1 = 0.536)). Excluding tenure yields a similar education coefficient ((_1 = 0.550)), confirming robustness. Results align with labor economics literature (e.g., Mincer earnings function).

Limitations

Omitted Variable Bias: Unobserved factors like innate ability or job type may inflate the education coefficient. Cross-Sectional Data: Cannot capture time-varying effects or establish causality definitively. 1976 Data: Results may not fully reflect modern labor markets.

Discussion

The analysis confirms a significant, positive effect of education on wages ($0.541 per year, p < 0.001), consistent with human capital theory. Experience and tenure also contribute positively, though with smaller effects. These findings support policies to enhance educational access. Future research could incorporate ability proxies or longitudinal data to address limitations. Communication of Findings

Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that education significantly boosts wages, with a $0.541 increase per year of schooling, robust to model specifications.
